# Inference chain: Moat & competitive position

**Goal:** What protects the economics, how durable that protection is, and what threatens it.

**Test each moat source explicitly (most small companies have at most one or two real ones):**
- **Switching costs:** embedded in customer workflow? long contracts? certified/qualified-supplier status (automotive, aerospace, medical, public sector)? Evidence: customer tenure claims, recurring revenue, framework agreements.
- **Local density / location:** the strongest small-company moat in services and hospitality. A care provider's borough density, a hotel's irreplaceable location, an installer's service radius. Cheap to verify, hard to replicate.
- **Regulatory/licence barriers:** licences, certifications (ISO, CQC registration, concessions) — barriers to entry, but also regulatory exposure. Both sides count.
- **Specialised know-how / niche scale:** "too small a niche to attract big competitors, too technical for casual entrants" — the classic Mittelstand moat. Test: how many credible competitors exist in the region? Would a customer qualify a new supplier easily?
- **Brand:** real for destination hospitality and consumer; usually weak B2B. Don't credit logos-on-website as brand.
- **NOT moats:** good service, nice people, "quality", being established. Name these as table stakes if the data offers nothing better — that honesty is informative.

**Durability & threats:**
- What kills this moat in 5–10 years? (technology shift, regulation change, key-person retirement, customer concentration, platform disintermediation — pick the real ones for this company.)
- **Owner dependence is a moat-destroyer:** if the moat lives in the founder's relationships/know-how, the company has a personal moat, not a corporate one. Flag explicitly — it is one of the most common small-cap deal-killers.
- Customer concentration: estimate from disclosed customers, case studies, procurement records. >25% single-customer is a deal risk; say if you cannot rule it out.

**Output:** the 1–2 real moat sources with evidence, a durability verdict (eroding / stable / strengthening), the top threats, and what this means for pricing power and margin sustainability. Feed the conclusion into the investment-angle section.
