# Inference chain: Exit / acquisition angle

**Goal:** Who would naturally buy this company, why, and what the realistic exit routes are. An investment without an exit thesis is a hope.

**Strategic acquirer mapping:**
1. List 2–4 *categories* of natural strategic acquirers and, where you genuinely know them, 1–3 named examples per category. Categories to test:
   - Horizontal consolidators in the same niche (who is rolling up this sector?)
   - Vertical players (customers buying capability, suppliers buying route-to-market)
   - Adjacent-product players buying the customer base or geography
   - International entrants buying market access (DACH access is a classic strategic rationale)
2. For each: the strategic logic in one sentence and what they would pay for (synergies, capability, multiple arbitrage). Never name an acquirer without the logic.

**Financial-buyer angle:**
- Is this a platform (scale, management depth, M&A pipeline) or a bolt-on (sub-scale, single-product, owner-dependent)? Bolt-ons trade at a discount but sell faster to sponsor-backed platforms — identify whether sponsor-backed consolidators are active in this niche.
- Sector multiple context: state a realistic EV/EBITDA range for the niche and size class (small private companies trade well below listed peers; sub-€2m-EBITDA businesses often 4–6×, €5m+ EBITDA niche leaders 7–10×, software higher). State the basis.

**Realistic exit routes ranked:** trade sale / sponsor sale / management buyout / family succession / no realistic exit (lifestyle business). Rank them for THIS company with reasoning. If the honest answer is "this is structurally hard to exit" (owner-dependent, customer-concentrated, asset-heavy with thin margins), say so prominently — it belongs in deal-killers.

**Output:** acquirer map, multiple range, ranked exit routes, and the implication for an investor today (what must be fixed before exit, e.g. de-risking owner dependence, audited numbers, second site).
